About Me

A small glimpse into my life.

  • Student

    I am a fourth year undergraduate student at the University of Pennsylvania. I am studying Mechanical Engineering and Applied Mechanics (MEAM), and I am also currently pursuing a Master's in MEAM with a focus on Heat Transfer, Fluid Mechanics, and Energy.

  • Aviation Enthusiast

    My main intellectual interest lies in the study of powered and non-powered flying machines. I enjoy studying everything from large commercial aircraft to human wingsuits and everything in between. My experience includes work with battery-operated quadrotors as well as several hours of flight under ram-air parachutes. Through my study of fluid mechanics, I hope to advance technology in aviation through better understanding of high Reynolds number aerodynamics.

  • Skydiver

    At a young age, I fell in love with the idea of human flight, so I began skydiving when I turned eighteen. I enjoy every aspect of the sport, from freefall to canopy piloting, and I am always trying to further develop my skills. One big goal of mine is to apply my love of engineering and innovation to the sport of skydiving to make it safer and more fun for everyone.

  • Musician

    Music and the arts have been a huge part of my life since my early childhood. I started playing piano when I was five years old, followed by guitar five years later. Rather than focusing on mastering one instrument, I have always aspired to be able to play any instrument that I might pick up. Currently, my musical focus has been on writing solo acoustic arrangements.

Press-fit Skyvan

MEAM 101: Into to Mechanical Design

This project was one of the first projects for MEAM 101, Intro to Mechanical Design Lab. All parts were modeled in Solidworks using parametric modeling. The assembly was press-fit together (no adhesive) and incorporated moving parts based on the design of a Short SC-7 Skyvan, an airplane commonly used in skydiving operations.

Da Vinci Crossbow

MEAM 101: Intro to Mechanical Design

This small-scale model of a Da Vinci-style crossbow was created for MEAM 101, my Mechanical Design Lab. All parts were modeled in SolidWorks as accurately as possible given Da Vinci's basic sketches, and the final product was laser-cut out of sheets of MDF and Acrylic. The crossbow was fully functional and could shoot a pencil around 15 feet after being wound up.

Click here to see the Da Vinci sketch that I used to model the crossbow to the (approximately) correct relative dimensions in SolidWorks.

Stirling Heat Engine

MEAM 201: Machining and Mechanical Design Lab

This butane torch-powered Stirling Heat Engine was created for MEAM 201, my Machining/Mechanical Design Lab. Each part was machined either manually or through CNC programming on provided mills and lathes. Some of the parts were provided in the form of engineering drawings while others came from personal SolidWorks design. During the final testing event at the end of the semester, my engine achieved a maximum rotational speed of 1100 RPM.

Vertical Axis Wind Turbine (VAWT)

MEAM 347: Junior Year Mechanical Engineering Design Lab

For my semesterly MEAM design lab, I teamed up with three other students to design and construct a vertical axis wind turbine. The main goal of the project was to run with the turbine at a constant speed and transfer the rotational energy of the shaft into a motor through a gearbox. Ultimately, we were trying to optimize the power generation of our turbine given our personal speed and size parameters.

At first, we built some small-scale prototypes of different turbine designs, including the Savonius turbine (beige in the pictures) and a version of our final Lenz design (pink in the pictures). After completing some fluid mechanics computations as well as experimental tests in the wind tunnel, we used dimensionless analysis to scale the design up to the final version. We constructed the turbine out of MDF, Acrylic, poster paper, and some steel wire rope. On testing day, our group achieved a power output of 11.34 Watts, the 4th highest power generation of 27 teams in the class.

Floating Arm Trebuchet

MEAM 348: Junior Year Mechanical Engineering Design Lab

The final project for my 2nd semester Junior MEAM design lab required me to join eight other students to build a machine of our choice. Each team was split into 3 sub-groups that were responsible for creating separate interfaceable components of the machine. Our team chose to build a floating arm trebuchet (see here to see one in action). Specifically, my 3-person sub-group was in charge of constructing the winding mechanism and ensuring that it would interface well with the other sub-groups' components.

We utilized SolidWorks during the design process, and we combined the results from SolidWorks stress analysis with similar COMSOL computations to determine the loads at which our gearbox would fail. On testing day, we interfaced all 3 sub-groups' components for the first time and successfully tested our trebuchet at a nominal load case and then a failure load case.

Modular Concert Stage

Ripe Concert

Together with friend and fellow student, Noah Shpak, I helped organize a successful philanthropy concert in our house. We handled all logistics for the show, including providing sound equipment for the 7-piece funk band, Ripe, and constructing a stable, removable stage.

The stage was built in 6 modular components, each consisting of an 8'x4' plywood sheet and several 2"x4" planks that we cut to size. Before finalizing the design, I did some basic stress analysis in SolidWorks to ensure that it would be able to withstand the weight of 7 people plus all of their equipment. Once built, the stage proved to be very sturdy and made for an awesome show.

I had the fortune of being able to test it firsthand when I played an opening set for the band (along with my friend Pierson Devers on bass guitar). Once we were finished, Ripe played an exciting, energetic set that involved a lot of jumping. In spite of the abuse that it withstood, the stage held together and was later disassembled and stored for future use.
